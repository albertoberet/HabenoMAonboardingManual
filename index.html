I. NON-NEGOTIABLE FUNDAMENTALS (The Core Rules)

Q1. LTV & Capital Cushion – The 40–45% Rule

Principle: Spanish banks finance 60–70% of the lower of purchase price or appraisal. Clients must provide the remaining 30–40% plus ~10–15% for taxes and fees.

Spanish banks typically finance between 60% and 70% of the lower value between the purchase price and the official appraisal (tasación). This means that if the property is valued lower than the agreed purchase price, the loan amount will be calculated based on the appraisal figure.

As a result, clients must contribute the remaining 30–40% of the property value from their own funds. In addition to this contribution, they should also budget approximately 10–15% extra to cover associated taxes, notary and registry fees, legal costs, and bank expenses.

This structure ensures that buyers demonstrate financial stability and commitment to the purchase while protecting banks from overexposure to market fluctuations. Junior advisors should always explain this clearly to clients early in the process to avoid misunderstandings about the total funds required for completion.

- Always calculate total cash required, including taxes, not just the down payment.
- Advise clients to maintain a currency buffer (5–10%) if funds are in foreign currency.
- Prepare clients for immediate bridging if appraisal is lower than purchase price.
✅ Verify client liquid funds.
✅ Calculate taxes and fees.
✅ Prepare LTV vs cash illustration.
✅ Recommend currency buffer for non-EUR funds.
"We can finance up to 70% of the property's value. To cover the remaining amount plus taxes, you will need approximately 40–45% of the purchase price in cash."
Example 1 – US client: Buying €400,000 apartment. Max bank loan 70% = €280,000. Down payment €120,000 + taxes €48,000 → total cash €168,000 (~42%).

Example 2 – UK client: Buying €250,000 villa. Max loan 65% = €162,500. Down payment €87,500 + taxes €30,000 → total €117,500 (~47%).

Q2. Debt-to-Income (DTI) – Avoiding the Silent Deal Killer

Principle: DTI must remain below 30–35% of net monthly income. Exceeding 35% triggers bank rejection.

The Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio is a critical factor for Spanish banks when assessing mortgage applications. It must typically remain below 30–35% of the client’s net monthly income to be considered safe and manageable.

Exceeding 40% of net income often triggers automatic rejection by the bank, as it signals excessive financial risk. This includes all monthly debt obligations, such as existing loans, credit card payments, and any other recurring financial commitments.

Junior advisors should calculate DTI early in the process and guide clients to adjust their borrowing expectations or financial profile accordingly. Explaining the importance of DTI to clients helps prevent surprises and ensures smoother approval of their mortgage application.

- Collect all global debts including mortgages, loans, and credit cards.
- Ensure transparency of all debts, including minimum payments.
- Use consistent calculation methods across nationalities.

✅ Advise client on debt reduction.
✅ Confirm bank DTI thresholds.

Q3. Property Appraisal (Tasación) – The Final Word

Principle: Bank loan is based on the lower of purchase price or appraisal. Client must provide extra cash if appraisal is lower.

In Spanish mortgage lending, the bank loan is always based on the lower value between the agreed purchase price and the official property appraisal (tasación). This ensures that the bank does not over-lend relative to the property’s market value.

If the appraisal comes in lower than the purchase price, the client must provide the difference in cash in addition to their planned down payment. This extra requirement covers the gap between the bank’s financed amount and the seller’s price, ensuring the transaction can proceed.

Junior advisors should review appraisal results carefully and communicate promptly with clients about any shortfall. Proper guidance helps clients prepare the necessary funds in advance and prevents last-minute surprises at closing.

- Advise liquidity for shortfall.

Q4. Self-Employed Clients – Proving Stability

Principle: Spanish banks require self-employed clients to demonstrate financial stability over a period of 3 years of fiscal activity. This usually involves providing tax returns, official income statements, and sometimes additional financial documentation to prove consistent earnings.

The bank will typically calculate an average stable income from the submitted years to determine affordability and risk. Fluctuations are reviewed carefully, as banks prefer clients with predictable, steady earnings.

Junior advisors should ensure self-employed clients gather all required documentation in advance, and explain how income will be assessed. This helps avoid delays or surprises during the mortgage approval process.

Q5. Source of Funds – AML Compliance

Principle: Spanish banks require that all funds used for property purchase are fully traceable to comply with Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regulations. This ensures transparency and legal compliance throughout the mortgage process.

Funds originating from cryptocurrencies or cash must be properly documented. Banks may require proof of conversion or transaction history and a period of “bank seasoning” of 3–6 months to demonstrate that the funds have been held in a legitimate bank account prior to the property transaction.

Junior advisors should guide clients in providing the necessary documentation early, explaining what constitutes acceptable proof of funds, and ensure funds are transferred well in advance of signing. Proper preparation prevents delays or complications during mortgage approval.

II. DOCUMENTATION ESSENTIALS (Bank-Ready Files)

Q7. ID, NIE & Tax Residency Proof

Principle: Each buyer must provide valid ID, NIE (Spanish tax number), and proof of current tax residency.

The bank verifies the authenticity and consistency of the documents. Any inconsistencies between the ID, NIE, and tax residency proof can cause delays in the approval process or even require resubmission of documents. For joint purchases, each buyer must provide their own documentation, and the bank reviews all files separately.

It is important to check the validity of passports (at least 6 months remaining at the time of signing) and ensure the NIE is correctly listed on all documents, including the purchase contract. Tax residency proof can be in the form of an official certificate, tax return, or equivalent document from the client’s home country. Foreign documents may require certified translation to meet bank standards.

Junior advisors should guide clients to gather these documents early, verify that all details are consistent, and ensure translations or certifications are complete. Proper preparation prevents delays, reduces stress for clients, and ensures the mortgage process proceeds smoothly.

- Passport must be valid at least 6 months after signing.
- Passport or NIE must appear correctly on all documents, including purchase contract.
- Proof of tax residency (tax return or certificate) is mandatory for all non-residents.
✅ Collect passport and NIE copies.
✅ Verify consistency across documents.
✅ Request tax residency certificate or latest tax return.
✅ Send to bank for compliance check.
"To process your mortgage, the bank needs your valid passport, NIE, and proof of tax residency in your home country."

The bank will carefully review payslips to ensure consistency in salary, bonuses, and deductions. Tax returns are used to confirm that declared income aligns with official records. Any discrepancies between payslips and tax returns may trigger additional questions or require clarification.

Junior advisors should ensure that clients collect all necessary payslips and tax returns before submitting the mortgage application. It is important to check that all documents are complete, legible, and cover the required time period. Missing or incomplete documents can delay bank approval.

For clients with variable income, such as those receiving commissions or bonuses, the bank may calculate an average monthly income to assess affordability. Advisors should prepare clients to provide supporting documents, such as bonus statements or employment confirmation letters.

Principle: Banks use internal credit scoring models to evaluate the risk of lending. These models consider multiple factors, including the client’s income, existing debts, type of property, nationality, residency status, and financial history.

The score determines the bank’s decision on approval, maximum loan amount, interest rate, and conditions. Non-resident clients or those with complex financial profiles may be subject to stricter scoring criteria. Clients with lower scores might receive reduced loan amounts or higher interest rates, while higher scores can facilitate smoother approval.

Junior advisors should explain to clients how these factors influence the mortgage outcome. Advisors can help clients prepare by ensuring that all documentation is complete, debts are accurately reported, and financial profiles are clear. Proper preparation improves the likelihood of a favorable score and reduces delays.

- Different banks weigh criteria differently (LTV, DTI, employment type).

- Non-resident applicants are usually assessed more conservatively.

8.DTI Calculation – Extended Scenarios for Non-Resident Clients

This section provides detailed examples of DTI calculation for non-resident clients, covering multiple nationalities and common debt situations.

  • Debts considered by Spanish banks: mortgages, rent, personal loans, car loans, leasing, alimony/child support, credit card minimums, legally binding obligations.
  • Net income calculation: monthly income after taxes and social contributions. Self-employed: average net income over 2–3 years.
  • Currency adjustment: Convert foreign currency to EUR, apply 5–10% FX safety discount.
  • DTI formula: DTI (%) = Total Monthly Debts ÷ Net Monthly Income × 100

Practical Examples
