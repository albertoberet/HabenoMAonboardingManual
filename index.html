Comprehensive training for Junior Mortgage Advisor guiding non-resident clients buying in Spain

I. NON-NEGOTIABLE FUNDAMENTALS (The Core Rules)

Q1. LTV & Capital Cushion – The 40–45% Rule

Principle: Spanish banks finance 60–70% of the lower of purchase price or appraisal. Clients must provide the remaining 30–40% plus ~10–15% for taxes and fees.

- Always calculate total cash required, including taxes, not just the down payment.
- Advise clients to maintain a currency buffer (5–10%) if funds are in foreign currency.
- Prepare clients for immediate bridging if appraisal is lower than purchase price.
✅ Verify client liquid funds.
✅ Calculate taxes and fees.
✅ Prepare LTV vs cash illustration.
✅ Recommend currency buffer for non-EUR funds.
"We can finance up to 70% of the property's value. To cover the remaining amount plus taxes, you will need approximately 40–45% of the purchase price in cash."
Example 1 – US client: Buying €400,000 apartment. Max bank loan 70% = €280,000. Down payment €120,000 + taxes €48,000 → total cash €168,000 (~42%).

Example 2 – UK client: Buying €250,000 villa. Max loan 65% = €162,500. Down payment €87,500 + taxes €30,000 → total €117,500 (~47%).

Example 3 – Sweden client: Buying €500,000 townhouse. Max loan 70% = €350,000. Down payment €150,000 + taxes €60,000 → total €210,000 (~42%).

Q2. Debt-to-Income (DTI) – Avoiding the Silent Deal Killer

Principle: DTI must remain below 30–35% of net monthly income. Exceeding 40% triggers bank rejection.

- Collect all global debts including mortgages, loans, and credit cards.
- Ensure transparency of all debts, including minimum payments.
- Use consistent calculation methods across nationalities.
✅ Gather all debt info globally.
✅ Calculate accurate DTI.
✅ Advise client on debt reduction.
✅ Confirm bank DTI thresholds.
"We include all your global debts to ensure your DTI is within bank limits. Any hidden or undisclosed debts can block approval."
Example 1 – UK client: €5,000 net/month, debts €1,500 → DTI 30% (acceptable).
Example 2 – Polish client: €6,000 net/month, debts €2,500 → DTI 41% (needs debt reduction).
Example 3 – UAE client: €12,000 net/month, debts €2,400 → DTI 20% (excellent).

Q3. Property Appraisal (Tasación) – The Final Word

Principle: Bank loan is based on the lower of purchase price or appraisal. Client must provide extra cash if appraisal is lower.

- Explain the impact of appraisal.
- Show tables with loan gap.
- Advise liquidity for shortfall.
✅ Confirm appraisal value.
✅ Calculate max loan.
✅ Show required extra cash.
✅ Include taxes/fees.
"The bank bases your loan on the appraisal. If it is lower than your agreed price, you must cover the difference immediately."
Example 1 – Sweden client: Buying €500,000, appraisal €480,000, 65% LTV → max loan €312,000. Client covers €168,000 + taxes €45,000 = €213,000 (~42.6%).
Example 2 – US client: Buying €400,000, appraisal €350,000, 70% LTV → max loan €245,000. Client covers €155,000 + taxes €42,000 = €197,000 (~49%).
Example 3 – UK client: Buying €250,000, appraisal €240,000, 65% LTV → max loan €156,000. Client covers €84,000 + taxes €28,000 = €112,000 (~44.8%).

Q4. Self-Employed Clients – Proving Stability

Principle: Banks require 2–3 years of fiscal activity, average stable income.

- Collect tax forms for 2–3 years (US: 1040 + Schedule C, UK: SA302, Poland: PIT).
- Average income, avoid peak year.
- Provide explanations for fluctuations.
✅ Collect tax documents.
✅ Calculate average net income.
✅ Ensure proof of stability.
✅ Prepare notes for bank if variable.
"We calculate your average income over 2–3 years to demonstrate stability for mortgage approval."
Example 1 – US client: 2022 $80k, 2023 $60k, 2024 $25 → average $55k.
Example 2 – Polish client: 2022 PLN100k, 2023 PLN90k, 2024 PLN95k → average PLN95k.
Example 3 – UK client: 2022 £50k, 2023 £55k, 2024 £75k → average £60k.

II. DOCUMENTATION ESSENTIALS (Bank-Ready Files)

Q7. ID, NIE & Tax Residency Proof

Principle: Each buyer must provide valid ID, NIE (Spanish tax number), and proof of current tax residency.

- Passport must be valid at least 6 months after signing.
- NIE must appear correctly on all documents, including purchase contract.
- Proof of tax residency (tax return or certificate) is mandatory for all non-residents.
✅ Collect passport and NIE copies.
✅ Verify consistency across documents.
✅ Request tax residency certificate or latest tax return.
✅ Send to bank for compliance check.
"To process your mortgage, the bank needs your valid passport, NIE, and proof of tax residency in your home country."
Example 1 – US client: Passport valid, IRS tax return + NIE copy accepted.
Example 2 – UK client: HMRC certificate used instead of tax return → accepted.
Example 3 – Swiss client: Old NIE expired → delayed until renewal.

Q8. Income Proof – Salaried Clients

- Translate if not in English or Spanish (sworn if necessary).
✅ Collect payslips (3–6 months).

✅ Cross-check income stability.
✅ Provide certified translations if required.
"The bank needs your last payslips and tax return to confirm stable income and ensure you meet mortgage affordability criteria."

Example 2 – Norway client: Skatteetaten summary + salary slips accepted.
Example 3 – UAE client: Employer letter + 3 payslips → manual verification required.

III. BANK ASSESSMENT & APPROVAL LOGIC

Q11. Credit Scoring – How Banks Evaluate Risk

Principle: Banks use internal scoring models considering income, debts, property type, nationality, and residency status.

- Different banks weigh criteria differently (LTV, DTI, employment type).
- Foreign income may be discounted (10–20%) depending on currency risk.
- Non-resident applicants are usually assessed more conservatively.
✅ Identify bank's scoring criteria.
✅ Provide complete financial documentation.
✅ Note currency or residency adjustments.
✅ Highlight stable income and low DTI.
"The bank evaluates each application based on income, debts, and property value. Foreign income may be adjusted slightly to account for currency risk."
Example 1 – US client: Stable income, 30% DTI → scoring 85/100 → approved.
Example 2 – UK client: Freelance income, 40% DTI → scoring 65/100 → conditional approval.
Example 3 – Sweden client: Resident, high savings → scoring 90/100 → smooth approval.

8.DTI Calculation – Extended Scenarios for Non-Resident Clients

This section provides detailed examples of DTI calculation for non-resident clients, covering multiple nationalities and common debt situations.

  • Debts considered by Spanish banks: mortgages, rent, personal loans, car loans, leasing, alimony/child support, credit card minimums, legally binding obligations.
  • Net income calculation: monthly income after taxes and social contributions. Self-employed: average net income over 2–3 years.
  • Currency adjustment: Convert foreign currency to EUR, apply 5–10% FX safety discount.
  • DTI formula: DTI (%) = Total Monthly Debts ÷ Net Monthly Income × 100

Practical Examples

Sweden – Employed

Income: SEK 60,000/month → €5,400 → €4,860 (10% FX discount)
Debts:
- Mortgage SEK 15,000 → €1,350
- Car loan SEK 3,000 → €270
- Credit card minimum SEK 500 → €45
Total debts = €1,665
DTI = 1,665 ÷ 4,860 ≈ 34.3% → acceptable

- Mortgage £1,200 → €1,380

Total debts = €2,185
DTI = 2,185 ÷ 6,555 ≈ 33.3% → acceptable

Key Notes & Recommendations

  • Always include all legally binding debts; missing items may lead to bank rejection.
  • Apply FX discount for foreign income (5–10%) depending on currency volatility.
  • Use average income over 2–3 years for self-employed clients.
  • Document all assumptions in Credit Act for transparency.
  • Alert clients if DTI is high, suggest debt reduction or larger down payment.
  • For borderline DTI cases (≈35–40%), consider choosing banks with more flexible policies.
